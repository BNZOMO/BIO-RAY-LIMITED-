Top 5 Home Security Mistakes

What we keep seeing during site visits in Nairobi, estates and apartments across Kenya — and how to fix it in a practical way.

Security tips • Home & small business

Quick overview: Most break-ins we investigate are not because of “sophisticated criminals” — they happen because of basic gaps. Use this list as a checklist when you walk around your home or apartment.

Mistake 1Relying only on a single lock or padlock

Many homes depend on one padlock at the gate or door. Once that lock is cut, there is nothing else slowing down entry.

We often see this in apartments with metal doors, rental bedsitters, and rural homes with simple wooden doors.

Better approach: Combine at least three layers:
  • Strong door/frame with internal deadbolt (not just padlocks outside).
  • Basic alarm or magnetic contact on the door that can trigger a siren.
  • CCTV watching the door/gate so activity is recorded and visible on your phone.
If you are on a budget, start with the most vulnerable entrance (usually the back door or main gate).

Mistake 2Poor or no outdoor lighting

Dark corners, side passages and parking areas give intruders confidence to move without being seen.

We see this a lot in townhouses and gated communities where common areas are lit, but private compounds are very dark.

Better approach: Use a mix of:
  • Motion-sensor lights near gates, doors and parking.
  • Solar floodlights to cover blind spots without adding wiring.
  • Positioning lights so they don’t shine directly into cameras.
With good lighting, your CCTV footage becomes much clearer and criminals are more likely to move on.

Mistake 3Ignoring blind spots in CCTV coverage

Cameras are only facing the front gate and sitting room, while intruders use side fences, back doors or rooftops.

During audits, we often find entire sides of the house, water tank areas or servant quarters not covered at all.

Better approach:
  • Walk around the compound and list all possible entry points (gate, doors, windows, fences, rooftop access).
  • Ensure every entry has at least one camera or is covered by a camera nearby.
  • Use wide-angle cameras on corners to cover two sides instead of placing cameras in the middle of a wall.
A short site survey from a professional can help redesign your layout without necessarily adding many new cameras.

Mistake 4Sharing travel plans and valuables online

Posting that you are “out of town for the weekend” or showing new electronics and cash on social media can unintentionally inform would‑be intruders.
Better approach:
  • Post trip photos after you return, not while you are away.
  • Share sensitive updates (e.g. travel, deliveries of electronics) only in private groups, not public timelines.
  • Combine this with simple physical measures: neighbour awareness, guard coordination and camera coverage.

Mistake 5Installing systems but never maintaining them

Cameras stop recording because the hard drive is full, alarm sirens fail, or extinguishers expire — but nobody notices until there is an incident.

We regularly find systems that were installed three or four years ago but have never been serviced.

Better approach:
  • Schedule a quarterly or bi‑annual maintenance visit with a service provider.
  • Test alarms, review camera recordings and confirm that night vision is working.
  • Log any changes (new tenants, new access points, renovations) and adjust coverage accordingly.
A simple maintenance contract is usually cheaper than replacing damaged equipment after a break‑in or fire.
Tip: Walk through this checklist with your family or staff. Assign one person to be responsible for “security housekeeping” every month.
