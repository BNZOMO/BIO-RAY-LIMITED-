Why this matters: Many premises in Kenya have extinguishers on the wall but staff are not sure which one to use — or the gauge is already in the red. This guide is written from real inspections we carry out in schools, offices and apartments.

Step 1Know your fire classes

Different materials burn differently. Using the wrong extinguisher can make a fire worse or put the operator in danger.

  • Class A – Ordinary combustibles: wood, paper, furniture, textiles.
  • Class B – Flammable liquids: petrol, diesel, paints, thinners.
  • Class C – Electrical fires: distribution boards, sockets, appliances.
  • Class D – Metal fires: magnesium, aluminium shavings (mainly industrial).
  • Class F/K – Cooking oils and fats: commercial and home kitchens.

Step 2Match extinguisher type to the risk

Below is a quick matrix we use when recommending extinguishers during site surveys.

Water

Best for Class A (offices, corridors, stores with paper and furniture). Never use on electrical or oil fires.

Foam (A/B)

Good for petrol stations, workshops, parking areas with fuel risk. Also works on many Class A fires.

CO₂

Ideal for electrical fires: server rooms, distribution boards, office electronics. Leaves no residue.

Dry Powder

Multi‑purpose option where you have mixed risks (A, B, C). Common for small businesses and compounds.

Wet Chemical

Designed for deep‑fat fryers and kitchens (Class F). Common in hotels, schools and restaurants.

Step 3Use the PASS method (and stay safe)

Before attempting to fight a fire, always raise the alarm, confirm you have a clear exit route, and never turn your back on the fire.

  • P – Pull the pin to break the seal.
  • A – Aim low at the base of the fire, not the flames.
  • S – Squeeze the handle to release the agent.
  • S – Sweep side to side until the fire is completely out.
Important: If the fire is bigger than you, spreading quickly, or filling the room with smoke, evacuate immediately and call the fire brigade. Equipment can be replaced — lives cannot.

Step 4Simple monthly safety routine

Once a month, walk through your premises and check:
  • Extinguishers are accessible, not blocked by furniture or stock.
  • The pressure gauge is in the green zone.
  • Service tag is valid and within the recommended service interval.
  • Exit routes and assembly points are clearly marked.
  • New staff know where extinguishers and alarms are located.
For schools, hotels and factories, we also recommend an annual fire drill.
