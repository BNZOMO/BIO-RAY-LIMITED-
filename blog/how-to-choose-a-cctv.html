How to choose a CCTV system

Use this checklist to decide camera types, placement, storage and budget — based on what we’ve seen on Kenyan sites.

Start with your objective: Do you want to scare intruders away, identify faces and number plates, monitor staff, or all of the above? Your answer will shape the design.

Step 1Pick the right camera types

  • Dome cameras – Discreet, vandal‑resistant and neat on ceilings. Great for offices, corridors and shops.
  • Bullet cameras – More visible and often longer‑range. Ideal for gates, fences and parking areas.
  • PTZ (Pan/Tilt/Zoom) – Moveable cameras used on larger sites like factories, estates or yards where a guard can actively monitor.

Step 2Think about resolution & night performance

Higher resolution is not always better if lighting and lenses are poor. For most homes and small businesses:

  • 1080p (2MP) – Good basic detail at close range; works for small compounds and indoor use.
  • 4MP–8MP (4K) – Better for wide areas, long driveways or where you need to zoom in after an incident.
  • Ask about low‑light or colour‑at‑night technology if your compound is dim or has many shadows.

Step 3Choose storage & retention

You need enough recording days to review incidents, but not so much that you pay for capacity you never use.

  • Local NVR/DVR – Common for homes and SMEs. Footage is stored on a hard drive at your premises.
  • Cloud / hybrid – Useful where theft of the recorder is a concern or you manage multiple sites remotely.
  • Most clients choose between 7–30 days of recordings, depending on risk and compliance.

Step 4Plan integration & remote monitoring

  • Check that your cameras can be viewed on a mobile app with secure logins.
  • Integrate with alarms, access control and intercoms so events trigger recordings and notifications.
  • Ensure your internet connection (and backup power) can support remote viewing if it’s critical for you.

Step 5Balance budget, quality & service

Two similar‑looking quotations can hide big differences in camera quality, cable type and support.

  • Ask for a clear breakdown of camera models, storage size and number of days of recording.
  • Confirm whether maintenance visits and training are included.
  • Remember: a slightly higher upfront cost is often cheaper than replacing failed equipment after one or two years.
Quick site checklist:
  • List all entrances, vulnerable windows and blind spots.
  • Decide which areas must have face‑level detail vs general overview.
  • Confirm how many days of recording you need.
  • Note where power and internet points are available.
